## CHAPTER 1: Before Lumera — The History of the Threaded World

### 1.1 — The Age of First Weaving

Nobody who was born in the current age of Asteria knows what the world felt like during the Age of First Weaving.

The scholars of the Covenant called that era the Lumen Period. The street preachers of Lumera called it the Before Time. The old women in the river district who hung charm threads from their window frames called it the age when the world was still deciding what it wanted to be.

They were all correct in ways that do not contradict each other.

Asteria in the Age of First Weaving was not a different place.

It was the same place with a different texture.

The Threads that now require specific people with specific abilities to perceive and touch were, in that era, simply present. Visible. Audible. Every living thing could feel the connections between itself and every other thing it had ever touched, loved, hated, feared, or needed. A farmer could feel the Thread between herself and the field she worked every day. A child could feel the Thread to a parent across any distance. A dying person could feel every Thread they had ever made, all at once, every relationship they had ever formed, lighting up at the end like lanterns being lit for a final celebration.

It was described by the texts that survived the collapse as beautiful.

It was also, as the texts do not admit but the evidence implies, unsustainable.

When everything is connected to everything else through living, feeling Threads, the emotional weight of the entire world is always present in some form. The grief of a mother in a distant city arrives faintly at the edges of your awareness. The joy of a celebration three villages over filters through like music from another room. Not loud enough to overwhelm. Loud enough to never be alone inside your own skin.

For most of the Lumen Period, this was manageable.

Then something happened that nobody has fully documented.

It is referred to in the surviving texts only as the Unraveling.

The texts do not say what caused it. Scholars have argued for centuries. The most common theory holds that one person, somewhere, experienced something so emotionally catastrophic that the resonance of it cascaded through the global Thread network the way a single crack cascades through glass under pressure. One break becoming many. Many becoming fracture. Fracture becoming collapse.

The competing theory, held by the older scholars who tend to look more uncomfortable when they say it, is that nothing caused it.

That the world simply reached a point where the weight of all that accumulated emotional resonance became more than the Thread network could hold.

And it came apart because it had always been going to come apart.

Whether by event or by inevitability, the result was the same.

The Threads went silent.

Not gone. Silent. Like a voice that has been shouting all its life suddenly losing the ability to speak above a whisper.

The world became quieter.

More separate.

And the people who had built their entire understanding of reality around the feeling of connection found themselves suddenly unable to feel it, with no warning, in the middle of whatever they were doing.

The grief of that silence is, according to the surviving accounts, the thing that broke the most people.

Not monsters. Not war. Not disaster.

The sudden absence of every Thread they had ever taken for granted.

### 1.2 — The Rise of Thread Seers and the Covenant's Birth

In the silence after the Unraveling, most people lost their connection to the Thread network entirely.

A small number did not.

They were scattered. Rare. One in several hundred, perhaps one in several thousand. People who, after the world went quiet, could still hear a faint signal. Not the full chorus of the Lumen Period. Something more like a single instrument playing where an orchestra had been. Faint enough to doubt. Present enough to follow.

They were called Thread Seers, eventually.

In the early years after the Unraveling, they were called other things. Most of those other names were not kind.

Thread Seers had two options for survival in a world that was afraid of what they represented. They could hide their ability and live quiet ordinary lives in the silence along with everyone else. Or they could find each other.

Enough of them found each other.

They formed the first Covenant.

Not the structure that governs Lumera and the other cities of Asteria now. That came later, and much of what it originally intended had been lost or deliberately changed by the time the current version was in place.

The original Covenant was simply a promise between Thread Seers.

We will not pretend we cannot hear what the world is saying.

We will listen together.

We will repair what we can.

For the first two centuries after the Unraveling, that promise held.

The Covenant grew slowly, carefully. It recruited Thread Seers through a network of quiet referrals. It developed the first organized system for understanding Thread mechanics: what Threads were, how they could be touched without being broken, what happened when they were damaged versus when they were severed, how to reinforce a weakened connection without flooding it.

More importantly, the early Covenant developed the first understanding of what the Thread network was actually holding back.

The documentation is incomplete. Several key texts were destroyed during the Covenant War three hundred years later. But the fragments that survive are consistent on one point.

The Thread network is not simply a feature of Asteria.

It is a membrane.

And on the other side of that membrane is something the world does not have a good name for. Something vast and old and entirely indifferent to the existence of anyone inside it. Something that does not press against the membrane with malice. Something that presses against it the way the weight of deep water presses against anything floating inside it.

Not because it wants to crush anything.

Because that is simply what weight does.

The early Covenant called the presence on the other side of the membrane the Deep Thing. Later scholars refined this to the Unnamed Presence. The street preachers of Lumera, who pay attention to more practical realities, call it the dark below the world. Liri, who is ten years old and asks extremely good questions, called it the big quiet when Mirika tried to explain it to her at the breakfast table and had to stop twice because the description kept making her feel like she was standing at the edge of something very high.

What it is called matters less than what it means.

The Thread network, every bond between every living thing in Asteria, holds the membrane stable. Every connection formed between two people, every friendship, every love, every shared grief, every remembered moment, strengthens the barrier between what Asteria is and what it would become if that barrier failed.

Every broken Thread weakens it.

This is not metaphor.

This is mechanics.

And it is why Velkor, who spent three hundred years systematically consuming the Threads of everyone who came near his prison door, was not just dangerous in the way that a very powerful enemy is dangerous.

He was dangerous in the way that a fire in the wall of a ship is dangerous.

The ship matters more than any one fire.

And the people trying to keep it floating matter more than any one fight.

### 1.3 — The Covenant War and What Came After

The Covenant War happened three hundred years before the events of Volume One.

The causes were complex. The short version that most people in Lumera learn is this: the Covenant grew, accumulated power, and then disagreed with itself catastrophically about what that power was for.

The longer version involves the discovery of Velkor.

The entity that would eventually be sealed in the forest prison door outside Lumera was not always sealed. He was, at one point, a Thread Seer. One of the most powerful the Covenant had ever documented. He was also the first person in recorded Asterian history to discover that a Thread Seer could consume a Thread rather than touch or reinforce it. That a connection between two people could be absorbed rather than honored. And that absorbing it generated power of a kind that simply touching a Thread never did.

Velkor proposed this discovery to the Covenant as a resource.

The Covenant refused.

Velkor left the Covenant and began consuming anyway.

What followed was not immediately a war. It was a series of disappearances. People who had strong Thread connections simply... stopping. Their bonds going suddenly dark. The people left behind feeling the absence of the connection without understanding why it was gone. Experiencing grief without being able to name its cause.

By the time the Covenant understood what was happening, Velkor had consumed enough Thread energy to be something that a Seer of normal ability could not approach safely. And he had found others like himself. Not many. Enough.

The Covenant War killed approximately one in four Thread Seers in Asteria.

It did not kill Velkor.

It took the five most powerful Thread Seers in the surviving Covenant working simultaneously for seven days to seal him into the stone prison in the forest outside what would eventually become Lumera. The seal used most of what those five people had. Three of them never Thread worked again. One died during the process. The fifth, a woman whose name was deliberately omitted from official records because the Covenant did not want her to become a symbol, lived another forty years and spent them making sure the seal held.

Her name was Ressa.

She does not appear in Volume One.

She is the reason Volume One can happen at all.

After the war, the Covenant rebuilt itself into something it originally had not been: a governing authority. Not just a network of seers who had made a promise to each other. An institution with laws and enforcement. A body that registered Thread Seers, licensed Thread work, and maintained physical control over the locations where the Thread membrane was thinnest.

The safehouse in Lumera where Meryn lives and heals is built directly above one of those thin points.

Meryn knows this.

She has not told anyone who did not already know.

## CHAPTER 2: The Geography of Asteria

### 2.1 — The World in Its Current Shape

Asteria is a world with three large landmasses, two inland seas, and one region that refuses to be mapped accurately.

The landmass where Lumera stands is called the Settled Continent by its inhabitants, which is the kind of name that reveals exactly how much they trust the continent to stay the way it is. It is temperate in the west, colder and more fractured in the north where the Thread folds are thickest, and warm and agricultural in the south where the thin soil over ancient Thread node convergence points produces crops that grow in unusual colors.

Lumera is a mid sized city by Asterian standards. White walls, red roofs, and a river running through its center that the locals call the Bright River during the day and something less polite at night when the shade beasts cluster near its banks. It is governed by a council of wardens who report to the Covenant Authority, which reports to a central body that most ordinary citizens of Lumera have never seen in person and are not sure they believe in.

The lower river district, where Sela works and Liri grew up, is the oldest part of the city. The buildings there are narrower and older. The streets are stone that has been walked on long enough to become smooth and slightly tilted. The Thread marker stones that the Covenant uses to monitor local Thread activity are older here too and some of them no longer report accurately, which the Covenant knows and has not fixed because fixing them would require admitting how thin the Thread membrane is under the oldest districts.

The forest north of Lumera contains the stone prison door where Velkor was sealed.

It also contains two other sealed structures that Yono does not know about yet.

Cadreth's file on Lumera's surrounding area has entries for all three.

He has not volunteered this information.

### 2.2 — The Three Pale Moons

When Yono wakes up in Asteria for the first time and looks at the sky, he sees three pale moons even though it is morning.

He notices. He does not mention it because he is already managing too much information.

The three moons are called, in the common tongue of Asteria, the Anchor, the Mirror, and the Wanderer.

The Anchor stays in a fixed position in the sky. It does not rise or set. Thread scholars believe it is the physical expression of the largest Thread node convergence in Asteria: a point of such dense Thread activity that it has pushed through into the visible sky as a permanent luminous body. The Anchor glows brightest during periods of high Thread activity. During the Covenant War it was, by multiple contemporary accounts, bright enough to cast shadows at noon.

The Mirror orbits normally. It reflects what the Anchor shows, but slightly delayed, which means that watching both moons at the same time creates the visual experience of seeing the present and the very recent past simultaneously. Thread scholars find this philosophically interesting. Normal people find it slightly nauseating after the first few minutes.

The Wanderer does not follow any orbital pattern anyone has successfully predicted. It appears in different positions every night. Sometimes it is visible during the day and invisible at night. Sometimes it is absent for weeks. Thread scholars have proposed that the Wanderer is not a moon in any conventional sense but a Thread entity large enough to be visible from the surface. The Covenant Authority has not officially confirmed or denied this, which most people who pay attention take as confirmation.

On the night that Yono seals Velkor back into the prison door and a voice that is not Velkor knocks back once from beneath the world, the Wanderer is in a position it has not occupied in three hundred years.

Mirika noticed.

She wrote it in her notebook and underlined it twice.

### 2.3 — Lumera at Night

There is a version of Lumera that the daytime does not show.

The market streets are loud and crowded and warm in ways that make you forget the city has a river district that burns sometimes and a forest north of the walls that hides things behind stone doors. The tea houses are lit from inside by Thread lamps that produce light in slightly warmer colors than regular fire. The guards at the gate are tired but performing confidence because people need to see confidence at a gate.

At night, Lumera is something different.

Not frightening exactly.

Honest.

The charms on the window frames catch the moonlight and glow faintly. The Thread markers in the older stones of the lower district pulse in rhythms that the long term residents have learned to read as warning or calm or uncertain the same way you learn to read someone's breathing pattern when you live with them long enough. The river at night looks darker than it is. The shadow on its banks that looks like something moving is sometimes just shadow and sometimes not and the residents have developed the skill of not looking directly at the parts they are not equipped to deal with.

Meryn works through the night more often than not.

Her stored breath jars are brightest in the hours before sunrise, which is when the people who have been hurting quietly all day finally arrive at her door with something that needs to be addressed.

Violet sits on rooftops sometimes.

Not because she needs to watch over the city in any formal sense.

Because rooftops are the only places in Lumera where the Thread signal from the Anchor moon is clear enough to hear without concentrating. She does not tell Yono this. She does not tell anyone. It is the thing she does when she needs to be reminded that the world is still working, still holding, still humming with the accumulated bonds of everyone in it, before she goes back inside and pretends she was just getting air.

## CHAPTER 3: What Shade Beasts Are and Where They Come From

### 3.1 — The Short Answer

Shade beasts are not animals.

They are not demons in the traditional religious sense. They are not the children of the Unnamed Presence. They are not accidents or mutations or the result of any natural process Asteria produces on its own.

Shade beasts are broken Threads.

Specifically they are Threads that have been severed so violently that the energy in them has not dissipated but has instead formed a kind of residual shape. A ghost shape. The outline of a connection that no longer exists, filled with the emotional resonance of whatever broke it.

A Thread broken by grief becomes something cold, heavy, and drawn to living warmth without being able to interact with it warmly. It presses against living things from outside, seeking the heat of existing bonds without being able to participate in them.

A Thread broken by violence becomes something aggressive. Fast. It does not have intention in any real sense. It has direction the way that a fire has direction: it moves toward whatever feeds it and moves away from whatever does not.

The shadow wolf that attacks Liri on the road outside Lumera is a Thread broken by violence. Its red mouth and cold leak are the visual signatures of a connection that was severed during something catastrophic, still carrying the imprint of whatever destroyed it.

When Yono fires three rockets into its chest and it comes apart, what he is actually doing is breaking the residual form enough that the energy finally dissipates. It scatters into the ash that then forms an eye shape and watches him.

That eye is not the wolf.

That eye is whatever severed the original Thread trying to identify what just destroyed its signature.

This is why Violet says faster when she sees it.

The eye is not a shade beast.

It is a question being asked by the entity that produces shade beasts.

And the entity now knows that Yono can provide answers.

### 3.2 — The Source of Shade Beasts

In Volume One's status updates, the antagonist directing the shade beast attacks is called the Threat Instruction Source.

This is Mirika's notation system, not an official name.

The Threat Instruction Source is not fully explained in Volume One. What can be established from the evidence the characters gather during Chapters One through Six is this:

It is organized.

The shade beast attacks on Lumera during the events of Chapter Two are not random. They target the river district specifically, which is both the most populated area of the lower city and the area directly above the oldest Thread node convergence point. The timing of the attacks correlates with Thread activity spikes that Mirika documents in her records.

Something is testing the membrane at its thinnest point.

Something that understands where the thin points are.

Something that has been watching Lumera long enough to know the Thread monitor stones in the lower district no longer report accurately.

The eye that forms from the wolf's ash and watches Yono before collapsing is the most direct evidence available: whatever orchestrates shade beast attacks registered Yono's existence and immediately began reassessing its strategy.

By Chapter Six, the constructs the Threat Instruction Source deploys are significantly more sophisticated than the shade beasts of Chapter Two. Not random broken Thread formations. Deliberately assembled. Engineered for specific objectives.

Someone is building soldiers out of broken bonds.

Someone who knows exactly what Threads to break to get the right shape.

The full identity of the Threat Instruction Source is the central mystery of Volume Two.

What can be said in this EX Chronicle without entering spoiler territory is that Cadreth knows who it is.

He has a file.

He has not volunteered this either.

## CHAPTER 4: The Covenant — What It Was, What It Became

### 4.1 — The Original Promise

The original Covenant was not a government.

It was not an authority. It was not a licensing body or an enforcement organization. It did not have wardens or officials or the kind of internal politics that currently requires anyone applying for a Thread work permit in Lumera to submit the application in triplicate and wait fourteen working days.

The original Covenant was a promise between Thread Seers who had found each other after the silence of the Unraveling and decided, together, that staying silent was not something they were willing to do.

We will hear what the world is saying.

We will repair what we can.

That was the whole document.

Two sentences, carved into a piece of stone that now sits in a locked basement under the Covenant Authority's administrative center in the capital city of Asteria, where it has not been shown to anyone in over a hundred years.

### 4.2 — What It Became

Governments do not arise because someone decides to create a government.

Governments arise because someone is making decisions and other people start letting them and eventually the letting becomes an expectation and the expectation becomes a structure and the structure grows walls and the walls eventually become the thing that matters most to the people inside them.

The Covenant Authority governs Thread work in Asteria.

It registers Thread Seers. It licenses Thread work operations. It maintains the Thread monitor stones throughout the settled continent and it controls access to the locations where the Thread membrane is thinnest, which it calls sensitive sites and does not explain further in public documentation.

It also has a classification system for entities that present risk to Thread membrane stability. The classification system has twelve tiers. Velkor, when he was active and consuming, was classified at tier eleven.

Yono Kazeshima is currently unclassified because the Covenant Authority does not yet know he exists.

When they find out, the classification meeting will take three days and end without consensus.

Cadreth, who attends these meetings as an observer without a formal role anyone has been able to define, will sit quietly in the corner and write in his book and say nothing useful until the meeting is nearly over and then say one sentence that changes the entire conversation.

This has happened seventeen times in the last two centuries.

Everyone who attends these meetings finds it deeply annoying.

### 4.3 — The Wardens of Lumera

The wardens who govern Lumera are not Thread Seers.

This is deliberate Covenant Authority policy: local governance positions are filled by non Thread workers to prevent any single city from developing an independent power base anchored in Thread ability. The wardens of Lumera can request Covenant Authority Thread teams for specific situations but cannot command them directly.

The guard Darin, who blocks the gate when Yono arrives with Liri during the Chapter Two events, is a warden soldier. He has no Thread ability. He has an order to close the gate. He has, underneath the order and the fear and the spear he is holding, a conscience that makes him feel bad about enforcing an order that keeps a child from her possibly dying mother.

Yono reads all of this in the time it takes to look at Darin's shaking hands.

Then he asks for ten seconds of bravery.

Not a lifetime. Not a heroic commitment. Ten seconds.

This is the thing about Yono that Cadreth eventually writes in his file as the most significant entry in an increasingly significant file.

He does not ask people to become something they are not.

He asks them to be what they already are for slightly longer than feels safe.

Most people can manage ten seconds.

Most of the time that is enough.

## CHAPTER 5: What Lives Beneath the World

### 5.1 — The Unnamed Presence

The Unnamed Presence does not have a goal.

This is the single most important and most frequently misunderstood fact about it.

When Velkor says come to the door through the black water at the shrine and his words enter Yono through the crescent in his palm rather than through his ears, that is Velkor using Thread channels to communicate. Velkor has goals. Velkor wants to be released. Velkor wants to consume. Velkor is, for all his ancient horror, an entity with desires.

The Unnamed Presence does not desire anything.

It simply is.

It exists on the other side of the Thread membrane in the way deep water exists below a swimmer. Not because it wants to be there. Not because it is waiting for something. Because that is its nature, and its nature does not include awareness of swimmers.

The pressure it exerts on the Thread membrane is not aggression.

It is weight.

The existential reality of the Unnamed Presence is not that it is malevolent. The existential reality is that it does not know Asteria exists. It does not know the people who love each other in Lumera's morning markets exist. It does not know that Liri tied a red ribbon around her wrist and called it a ward and that the ward works partly because she believes in it with the complete and absolute belief that only a ten year old who has just lost her father and needs something to trust can generate.

None of that is visible from where the Unnamed Presence is.

The Thread membrane keeps it from seeing.

The Thread membrane keeps Asteria from dissolving into the vast indifference on the other side of it.

Every bond matters.

Including the one Yono makes with Liri on the road to a burning city, when he holds out his smallest finger and says yes you can borrow my strength until you find yours again.

That Thread is real.

It strengthens the membrane by a tiny, unmeasurable, absolutely real amount.

### 5.2 — What Velkor Was Doing

Velkor was not trying to contact the Unnamed Presence.

He does not have a manifesto. He does not have a plan to destroy Asteria. He is not aligned with any cosmic force or working toward any ideological goal.

Velkor was simply hungry.

He had been hungry for three hundred years in a sealed stone prison with no access to the Threads he needed to sustain himself. The entities that feed on Threads degrade without them. They do not die in the way that mortals die. They diminish. They become something less than what they were, without becoming nothing. Velkor had spent three centuries becoming less and less of himself while remaining aware enough to resent it completely.

When he finally senses Yono approaching, when the crescent in Yono's palm resonates with the geometry of the prison seal, Velkor recognizes something that has not happened in a very long time.

A Thread he has not already consumed.

Not just any Thread.

A Thread connected to something extraordinary.

He calls Yono little crown because he can smell the depth of what Yono actually is underneath the ordinary shape of a nineteen year old from Tokyo who is still thinking about a cream bun he never got to eat.

He says buried under lies not as an insult but as an observation.

He is correct.

The lies are not someone else's lies.

They are the story Yono tells himself about what he is.

That story, and why Yono chose to believe it, is the subject of Chapter Eleven.

### 5.3 — The Thing That Knocked Back

When Yono presses two fingers to the prison door after defeating Velkor and writes the rule this door will not use children into the stone using Thread Cut and Rule Maker in combination, something knocks back.

Once.

Politely.

This is not Velkor. The volume makes this clear. This is not the Unnamed Presence either. The Unnamed Presence does not knock politely on doors. The Unnamed Presence does not knock at all.

What knocks back is something that has been aware of the prison seal for three hundred years and has been watching it with interest.

It is not the Threat Instruction Source. It does not want Yono to come to a door or serve a purpose. Its interest in him is genuine curiosity of a kind that is entirely alien to human emotion but which, if translated into the closest available human equivalent, would come out as something like:

There is a variable I have not encountered before.

I would like to see what it does next.

Mirika, who goes pale when she hears the knock, describes it later in her notebook as follows:

Old. Aware. Not aligned with anything I have catalogued. The politeness of the knock is not social courtesy. It is precision. It knocked exactly once at exactly the right volume to be heard without startling. Whatever knocked has very fine control over the force it chooses to apply. That level of control implies immense capability being deliberately restrained.

She underlines the word deliberately.

She underlines it twice.

Then she closes the notebook and does not open it again until morning.

## CHAPTER 6: The Thread System — A Complete Record

### 6.1 — What Threads Actually Are

A Thread is a bond made visible.

This is the starting point and also the full description and also, maddeningly, not enough information to work with in practice.

Every connection between two things that have mattered to each other creates a Thread. Not just emotional connections. Physical ones too, in the sense that things which have been in sustained contact develop a faint Thread signature, though it is thin and unreliable. Conceptual connections: the Thread between a person and a belief they hold deeply is as real as the Thread between a person and someone they love, though it has a different signature and behaves differently under stress.

The strongest Threads are the ones that have survived something.

A bond that has been tested by loss and held is significantly stronger than a bond that has never been tested at all. The Thread between Yono and Liri, formed during the road to Lumera under active threat and grief, is disproportionately strong for its age because it was made during the kind of moment that either breaks a connection or tempers it into something that does not break easily.

This is one of the reasons Yono's ability to generate Thread resonance grows faster than any developmental timeline the Covenant Authority has on record.

He keeps choosing bonds in circumstances that temper them immediately.

He does not do this strategically.

He does not know this is happening.

He is simply the kind of person who, when standing on a road with a scared child who has just lost her father, holds out a hand instead of explaining why holding out a hand is complicated right now.

The Threads answer accordingly.

### 6.2 — Thread Perception

Most people in Asteria cannot perceive Threads at all.

They live inside the Thread network without seeing it the way a person lives inside air without seeing it. The connections are there. The bonds are real. The strengthening or weakening of the membrane is happening all around them every day. They simply lack the sense required to perceive any of it directly.

Thread Seers develop this sense.

The form it takes varies by individual. For most registered Seers in the Covenant Authority's records, Thread perception is primarily visual: Threads appear as lines of light in their peripheral vision, with color signatures that correspond to Thread type. Emotional bonds appear in warm colors. Temporal Threads (connections between a person and a past moment) appear in cooler, more silver tones. Conceptual Threads tend toward deep blue or violet. Hollow Threads, which are severed connections whose resonance has not yet dissipated, appear in colors that do not quite correspond to the standard spectrum and tend to make most Thread Seers uncomfortable in ways they cannot fully articulate.

For Yono, Thread perception comes through the crescent mark in his palm. He does not see Threads as lines of light. He feels them as information arriving through his hand. Temperature, pressure, resonance pattern. He understands them before he can describe them. When Velkor's voice enters through the shrine water and his crescent translates it, Yono does not hear words in the conventional sense. He receives the intention behind the words directly, which is simultaneously more accurate and more disturbing than simply hearing language.

For Violet, Thread perception is proprioceptive. She knows where every Thread connected to her is at all times without needing to look for them. She also has a limited but reliable ability to sense the Thread state of people in her immediate vicinity, which is how she knew, before she told Yono to stop moving, that his regeneration was masking a wound he was still actively losing blood from.

She noticed the Thread between him and his own physical continuity getting quieter.

She did not explain this.

She just told him to sit down.

### 6.3 — Thread Work in Practice

The formal categories of Thread work established by the original Covenant are as follows:

**Sensing:** Basic Thread awareness. The passive perception of Thread presence in one's environment without active engagement.

**Reading:** Active interpretation of Thread content. Understanding not just that a Thread is there but what it connects, what its emotional signature is, how old it is, how strong. Thread reading is used in healing to assess a patient's relational health, in investigation to reconstruct the emotional history of a location, and in navigation to identify Thread fold zones where the local membrane is thin.

**Touching:** Direct contact with a specific Thread without disrupting it. The rarest and most valued skill at the lower practitioner level because most people who reach for a Thread accidentally push or pull it. Touching requires a gentleness of intention that most people have to deliberately develop and cannot maintain under stress.

**Reinforcing:** Strengthening an existing Thread by channeling authentic emotional resonance into it. What the Weavers who work with the Covenant Authority do for people whose bonds have been damaged by exposure to shade beasts or Hollow Thread zones. Requires genuine emotional engagement: you cannot reinforce a bond with technique alone. The resonance has to be real.

**Building:** Creating new Threads deliberately. The hardest formal skill because it requires both parties to a bond to be emotionally present to the connection being formed. You cannot build a Thread for someone who is not willing to have one. This is why Thread coercion is classified as a serious violation of Covenant law: you can force proximity but you cannot force the genuine emotional presence that makes a Thread real.

**Cutting:** Severing a Thread. This is where Thread Cut as a named ability differs from the ordinary cutting that any practiced Thread worker can perform in emergencies. Ordinary Thread cutting requires physical proximity to the Thread being severed and carries the emotional cost of participating in a severance: the person doing the cutting feels the breaking of the bond in some degree. Thread Cut, Yono's recovered ability, is something categorically different. See Chapter Ten.

## CHAPTER 7: The Seal System — Yono's Black Hall Explained

### 7.1 — What the Black Hall Is

The black hall is not a metaphor.

When Yono first catches a glimpse of it in the white room of divine administration after his death, he sees: a dark hall. No walls. No ceiling. Gold cords hanging into forever.

This is an accurate visual representation of something real inside him. Not inside his body in any physical sense. Inside the part of him that exists at a level deeper than the physical.

Every gold cord hanging in the hall is a sealed version of something he used to be able to do.

Used to be.

Because Yono is not new.

This requires explaining carefully because it is the central fact of his character and it is also the fact he is most consistently not thinking about.

### 7.2 — The Crown That Was Buried

The thing that Velkor smells on Yono, that he calls little crown, is not a title.

It is a description of what Yono actually is at the level below the level that died on a Tokyo bridge and woke up in the white room with Violet.

The full nature of that deeper level is something Volume One is not yet ready to fully reveal. What can be established at the EX Chronicle stage is this:

Whatever Yono is at his deepest level chose, at some point before the events of Volume One and before the events of his Tokyo life, to become small.

Not diminished. Not defeated. Not broken.

Chosen.

The seals in the black hall were not placed by an external force. They were not a punishment or a containment. They are decisions. Each gold cord hanging in the hall is one Yono tied himself. Each seal is a version of him that he put away deliberately.

Why would something capable of rewriting the rules of reality choose to become a nineteen year old in Tokyo who fantasized about being important while doing nothing important and spent his evenings eating cheap convenience store food and planning to eventually fold his laundry?

Because the laundry was his.

Because the convenience store was a choice nobody else made for him.

Because being vast and alone is not the same as being strong.

The black hall is full of cords he sealed because he was trying to find something that mattered before he was enormous again. Something small enough to fit on a park bench. Something that could hold an orange and laugh with an old woman about how neither of them could bend properly in the cold.

He found Lumera.

He found Liri and Sela and Meryn and Mirika and Lyra and the safehouse that smells like ginger and old magic.

He found Violet, who pushed him off a bridge and then stood beside him in every mess after.

The cords are breaking because the reasons are compounding.

Every chapter. Every choice. Every moment he decides to stay human instead of becoming something that cannot be spoken to over a breakfast table.

A seal breaks.

A cord releases.

The latest Yono becomes the strongest Yono.

And last chapter's ceiling becomes the new floor.

### 7.3 — The Rule of Seal Breaking

The seals break according to a rule that Yono did not consciously design but that reflects something true about how he works.

A seal does not break because Yono is strong enough.

A seal breaks because Yono has a good enough reason.

This is why the Covenant Authority's files on Thread Seers and their developmental trajectories have no category for him. Their entire framework for predicting Thread practitioner growth is based on the assumption that power scales with ability: the stronger the practitioner, the more they can do, and the more they practice, the stronger they get.

Yono does not work like this.

Yono's power scales with meaning.

He broke the first seal in the forest clearing because Liri asked him to bring everybody home and the weight of trusting an adult who was not sure he deserved it yet was heavier than the seal.

He broke the second seal when Velkor tried to use the children as leverage and the part of Yono that had lived long enough to know what things cost decided that the cost of staying sealed was no longer acceptable.

The third seal, the one that is pressured and aware and waiting at the end of Chapter Six, is waiting for a reason that has not yet arrived.

When it does, it will not be a battle.

It will be a decision.

### 7.4 — What Breaking Feels Like

Yono describes the seal breaking experience twice in Volume One.

When Mirika asks what it is like to break a seal, Yono says: something that was always yours coming back. Not new. Just returned.

When Violet asks if it feels like what she experienced when her inner door opened further, Yono says: like walking in a smaller version of yourself and then stopping because someone needed more and more was there.

The second description is more accurate but only makes sense in the context of what Violet understood when she said it back to him.

What it actually feels like, from the inside, during the moment of breaking:

The hall gets one step shorter.

Not brighter. Not louder. One step shorter. The specific cord that is releasing comes loose with something that is not pain and not relief, but the particular sensation of something that has been held in tension for a very long time finally being allowed to rest. And then whatever was behind that cord is simply present. Available. Not new. Familiar in the way that a room you grew up in is familiar when you walk back into it after years away. You did not forget it. You just could not be in it until now.

The power that comes back with each broken seal is not a new tool.

It is a remembered one.

Yono uses Thread Cut after the second seal breaks and he uses it with the immediate fluency of someone who has done it ten thousand times because he has done it ten thousand times. He simply did not have the memory of it accessible until the seal released.

This is why he says I know after using it in ways that should require extensive practice.

He already knew.

He sealed it away.

Now it is back.

Violet's power system is structurally different from Yono's.

Unexamined power.

The parallels between Violet's power structure and Yono's are not accidental.

A goddess of flowers who also carries a concept of divine verdict, whose true power level was enough to make Cadreth block for the first time in what was clearly a very long time, does not arrive at a cautious, measured, small expression of her abilities by accident.

She has witnessed a great deal of what mortals do when they are afraid and hurting and hope is something they are running low on.

She has seen the full version of herself frighten the mortals she was meant to help.

She has seen what happens when a divine being at full expression stands in a room with people who are already overwhelmed.

Not weaker. Not smaller in the important sense.

Just less of a ceiling than the people she was walking with needed her to be.

She calibrated herself to the room.

When the room changed, when the room became Yono, when Yono turned out to be someone whose ceiling she could not find because the concept of a ceiling did not fully apply to him, the calibration became unnecessary.

The door opened more because the person standing next to her was not frightened of what was behind it.

He was, if anything, curious.

That curiosity, in the absence of fear, is what allowed her to finally examine the drawer.

### 8.3 — Bloom Absolute

Bloom Absolute is the formal name for what happens when Violet releases multiple concepts simultaneously.

Yono notes it in the way he said oh quietly, the way he said it when the black hall turned out to be longer than he could see.

He recognized what it meant.

She had been choosing to be smaller than this.

For him.

And now she was not choosing that anymore, and he was the reason.

## CHAPTER 9: The Rule Maker Mechanism

### 9.1 — What Rule Maker Is

Rule Maker is listed in the compensation menu Violet presents to Yono in the divine administration white room, under the heading one major ability package.

Yono selects Time Control and Regeneration.

Rule Maker is not on the menu.

Rule Maker arrives during Chapter Three when Yono stops a hunger wave from reaching Violet by removing its permission to arrive. It is the ability that makes Mirika say that was not time control and Yono respond I know with a calm that is slightly too complete for someone discovering a new ability.

The calm is not pretended.

The ability is not new.

Rule Maker is the first recovered ability that does not correspond to a specific seal breaking. It appears earlier than expected because it is not sealed in the traditional gold cord sense. It is something closer to a reflex than a skill. Something so fundamental to what Yono is that the sealing of it was never total. It exists at a level below the hall.

What Rule Maker does, formally:

It allows Yono to identify a rule, property, or characteristic that currently applies to a situation and revise it.

The wave had a rule: it was permitted to spread outward from the prison door.

Yono removed the permission.

The wave did not know what to do with this.

Nothing had ever removed its permission before.

The wave did the only thing it could in the absence of permission.

It stopped.

In Chapter Five, Yono uses Rule Maker to rewrite the part of the Covenant authority's commands around the prison door where they had ever outranked him. The seal around the door turns inside out and obeys its newer master.

This is not Yono fighting the seal.

This is Yono correcting a factual error in the seal's premise.

The seal operated on the rule that Covenant authority outranked everything present at its location.

This rule was simply wrong, once Yono was standing at its location.

He did not argue with it.

He corrected it.

The difference between those two approaches is the reason Cadreth is interested.

### 9.2 — The Limits of Rule Maker

Rule Maker is not unlimited.

The revision Yono applies to a rule has to be honest. He cannot rewrite a rule to something factually untrue. When he presses his palm to the prison door and writes this door will not use children into the stone, this rule holds because the door's behavior can be made consistent with that rule without the rule being false.

This door will not use children is not a wish. It is a declaration of a new operating parameter.

But Yono could not write Velkor is no longer hungry into the stone and have it hold. That would require changing something true about Velkor's nature, not redirecting a behavioral parameter. Rule Maker works on rules, not on realities. The distinction is subtle but significant.

It also has a cost.

Every significant application of Rule Maker requires Yono to be entirely certain about the revision he is making. Not emotionally certain. Factually certain. If there is genuine ambiguity in the rule he is trying to apply, the mechanism does not work. It produces nothing. This is why Rule Maker in practice requires the specific clarity of intention that most of Yono's most effective moments share: he does not act while still deciding what the right action is. He decides first. Then he acts from the decided position.

This is also why he sometimes seems slightly slow to react in situations with unclear morality.

He is not slow.

He is deciding.

## CHAPTER 10: Thread Cut — The Forbidden Separation

### 10.1 — What Thread Cut Is

Thread Cut is not ordinary Thread severance.

Ordinary Thread cutting, as performed by practiced Thread workers in emergency situations, requires proximity to the Thread being severed, conscious knowledge of what you are cutting, and the emotional cost of participating in a severance. You feel it. The bond breaking is not neutral for the person cutting it.

Thread Cut removes all of those requirements.

Yono can identify the link between a force and its purpose, between an attack and its intent, between a concept and its active application, and sever that link at the level of relationship. The force does not disappear. It becomes directionless. It loses the relational connection to its own meaning.

When Cadreth releases a concept erasure wave designed to remove the premise of resistance, Yono cuts the link between the wave and its purpose. The wave arrives, vast and cold. But it has no purpose anymore. So it does nothing. It passes through the room like wind with no temperature and dissipates against the far wall as if it has forgotten what it was for.

This is Thread Cut applied to an abstract attack.

It works on physical attacks too. In Chapter Two, during the fight with the Warden, Yono uses an early and unstable version of Thread Cut to disconnect the Warden's coordinated assault sequence: he cuts the Thread between one movement and the next, creating a gap in the momentum of the attack sequence that should not physically exist.

### 10.2 — Why It Was Sealed

Thread Cut is in Cadreth's archive.

Cadreth says the file lists it as permanently sealed.

He says it was owned by one entity in the old archive.

He does not say when it was sealed.

He does not say why.

But the implication in the way he says it, the specific weight of permanently as opposed to any other temporal modifier, suggests that the sealing was not a temporary measure. Not a safety restriction pending review. A decision intended to be permanent.

A decision Yono then simply... reversed.

By breaking the seal himself.

Because it was his seal.

He placed it.

He unsealed it when the reason not to have it became smaller than the reason to.

Cadreth's response to learning this, the almost pleased expression he wears when he says your file is not keeping up with you, is the response of someone who has found a record that significantly updates his understanding of what he is dealing with.

The permanent seal was in the archive as evidence that the entity who owned Thread Cut had placed it themselves.

What the archive did not have a category for was an entity that had sealed their own permanent ability seals and then unsealed them because they found better reasons.

The archive is being updated.

## CHAPTER 11: Yono Kazeshima — The Crown That Chose to Be Small

### 11.1 — Before the Bridge

Yono Kazeshima was nineteen years old and largely unremarkable from the outside.

He had an apartment in Tokyo that was too small for the amount of laundry he generated and the number of books he bought from discount bins outside closed shops. He talked to the cream bun.

He tried to save the cat.

Not because he had calculated the risk.

Because it was in danger and he was there and he could not look away from something that needed help just because looking away would have been safer.

He found it.

Now the seals are breaking because the people he found are real enough to be worth the uncovering.

### 11.3 — His Relationship With Being Afraid

Yono tells Liri on the road to Lumera, with complete honesty, that he is scared. Not performing bravery. Not minimizing the fear for her benefit. Telling a ten year old who has lost her father that the person she is borrowing strength from is also afraid.

Then he carries her toward the burning city anyway.

He acts afraid.

This is why the Unnamed Presence, for all its vast indifference to the existence of anything in Asteria, would register Yono if it could register anything.

Not because he is powerful.

Because he is present.

Fully, completely, stubbornly present in a way that makes the Threads around him sing at a frequency that the oldest architecture of the world recognizes as significant.

He is, in the old language that no one in Asteria currently speaks, the word that means someone who stayed when they could have left.

### 11.4 — Yono and Violet

This section is being written carefully because the characters involved are complicated about it.

Yono reaches for Violet's hand the way he reaches for everything: without calculation and with complete sincerity. She is there. He is there. The connection is real. He does not perform it or manage it or approach it as a strategic element of his situation.

What grows between them is earned by everything the story costs them.

She is managing it.

He knows she knows.

He lets her manage it in peace.

This is, for Yono, the simplest form of respect.

He does not push.

He exists, right there, like that, and she said it herself:

It is extremely irritating.

## CHAPTER 21: How Routes Work in Threadborn

### 21.1 — What an IF Route Is

An IF Route is not an alternate timeline.

The Threadborn IF Routes are not parallel worlds or hypothetical realities or the kind of narrative device that implies the main story is arbitrary. They are something more specific: they are the six distinct trajectories the story can follow depending on which bonds Yono builds most deeply, and what choices he makes at the critical decision points the story places in front of him.

The world has one timeline.

The IF Routes are six different paths through it.

Most of them reach the same destination via different roads. The road changes what survives the journey.

### 21.2 — Critical Decision Points

Each IF Route branches from one of three critical decision points:

**The First Decision:** Who does Yono choose to be present for most fully during the period after the forest clearing fight? His answer to this question shapes which bond deepens most in the weeks following.

**The Second Decision:** When the next sealed door inside the black hall presents itself, what does the seal tell Yono is behind it? Each route has a different answer.

**The Third Decision:** When the Threat Instruction Source reveals itself and offers Yono something that would be valuable to one of the people he cares about at a significant personal cost, does he consider it?

Routes branch from Decision One. Routes close at Decision Three.

The True Route requires specific answers to all three decisions that cannot be reached by trying for the True Route specifically. It can only be reached by being consistently honest about what matters most.

She watched him put the bag down and hold out his hands to a cat he did not know in a way that contained everything the Thread had promised her was real about him.

She did not mean to push him.

She reached out to stabilize the moment because he was about to fall on his own and she did not want the Thread to break against asphalt on a rainy Tuesday in Tokyo, not when it had waited this long to find the right conditions to become something.

She miscalculated the force.

He went over the rail.

The look on her face, the oh no of it, which he heard through the dying fog and thought was disappointingly small for the end of a life, was the specific face of someone who had spent centuries being careful and had just, in one moment of genuine panic, done the catastrophic thing she was carefully not doing.

She stood there looking at the damage she had caused.

She had pushed someone off a bridge.

She had killed someone.

She had killed the person whose Thread she had been watching for centuries because she was afraid of losing the moment and she grabbed too hard.

The Divine Administration cube found her standing there with her hands still outstretched.

She did not argue with the assignment.

She accepted court ordered accompaniment not because the Administration demanded it but because she had decided the moment the sound came from below the bridge that she owed him the road.

All of it.

However long it was.

She is paying it.

She is not sure it feels like debt anymore.

## CHAPTER 29: Side Story — The Black Hall Before Yono

The black hall existed before there was a Yono.

Before Tokyo. Before Asteria. Before the specific human form currently walking around Lumera with a tennis racket and a crescent in his palm.

The hall was the same. Gold cords. No visible ceiling. The patient darkness of something that had been holding itself in deliberate storage for a very long time.

What changed is the light at the entry point.

Before Yono, the hall's entry was visible to nothing. No one standing at the entry could perceive it. The hall existed inside what it existed inside without any interface to the level of reality that ordinary people move through.

The first change was the park bench.

The old woman's Thread to the husband she had lost that spring was a Thread Yono sat beside and felt, without knowing he was feeling it, through ten minutes of cheap coffee and shared silence. The hall's entry point warmed by one degree.

The convenience store. Tuesday evenings. The woman who always bought the same brand of crackers and said thank you like she meant it every single time. The man who asked the same question about his lottery numbers and laughed the same way every time at the answer. The small regular human Threads that Yono was a constant presence beside.

The hall warmed.

Not dramatically. Not visibly. Not in a way that anyone including Yono could have noticed or measured.

Just warmer.

The bridge happened.

The hall went cold for one moment. The specific cold of a Thread suddenly under threat of breaking. And then the hall registered the same Thread it had been warming toward for months arriving in the divine administration white room in the shape of a young man saying am I dead in a voice that was asking about more than the immediate answer.

The first gold cord that would later break was the warmest one.

Because it had been the longest in storage.

The hall remembered what it was storing it for.

### The First Cord

The first sealed cord corresponds to the ability that Yono uses in Chapter Three to stop the hunger wave.

It was the last thing he sealed before becoming small.

Not because it was weak. Because it was the one he was most afraid of keeping.

He was afraid that if he kept the ability to remove permission from things, he would start removing permission from everything. That something that can say you are not allowed to arrive at someone I care about would eventually become something that says no one is allowed to arrive at me at all.

He sealed it before he found out whether that fear was true.

In Chapter Three, when Violet is in the path of the wave and he removes its permission to arrive, he finds out.

The fear was not true.

He used the ability to protect someone else.

Not to protect himself from having to feel anything.

The cord released.

He said I know.

He did know.

He had been knowing it for a very long time on the other side of the seal.

## CHAPTER 30: Side Story — Meryn and Rell

Rell was eight years old.

He had a specific way of pronouncing her name that made both syllables take slightly longer than they needed to because he was still learning how to make the sounds she made naturally and he was practicing at full effort.

He had opinions about breakfast that he expressed with great confidence. He preferred mornings that started slow. He was bad at patience during the slow parts because he was eight. He had brown eyes and he had a laugh that started too big and then got quieter the longer it went, and she used to try to make him keep laughing just to hear the quiet version.

He was eight when the healer had nothing left.

She was sixteen and she stood in a room and understood the mechanics of what was happening before she understood the grief of it.

She understood the grief later.

It arrived later that night, after the adults had handled the immediate requirements and she was alone in the room that still had his breakfast preferences on the table, the specific cereal he wanted slowed down for, the cup sized for small hands.

She took everything out of the room.

Not to erase him.

To protect herself from the inventory of his specifics becoming the only thing she had left of him.

She became a healer.

She became the kind that saves pieces of herself in small glass jars so there would always be something left when someone arrives too late.

She has never arrived too late.

Not since.

She does not use the word never.

She uses so far.

So far means she is still counting. Still working. Still in the space where the jars are full enough and the people come and she has something to give.

So far means Rell is still the reason.

She puts warm stored breaths in the jar she keeps in the spot that was always empty.

She does not call it his spot out loud.

She calls it the reserve.

The reserve is for when so far ends.

So far, so far has not ended.

## CHAPTER 31: Side Story — Mirika Outside the Circle

She remembers the sound first.

Not the seal breaking. Not her father's voice stopping. Not her mother saying who are you talking about when she said father three days later.

The sound of the stone floor outside the circle. She had been cold. She stepped outside the circle because she was cold and the circle drew heat for the working. One step outside. The stone was cold under her feet and she turned to go back in and the seal went wrong and she watched from outside.

She was outside.

That is the whole story.

One step outside because she was cold.

She does not know if she would have survived if she had been inside the circle.

She does not know if she would have preferred that.

She has not asked herself directly.

She has come close. In the notebook with the warmer ink. She has started the question and then written something else. The something else has been, for twelve years, the accumulated record of her father: his specific way of making morning tea (two spoons, no sugar, stir counterclockwise once), his hands (wide, with a specific callus on the right thumb from pressing his pen too hard), his voice (lower than expected for his face), his opinions about breakfast (aligned, Mirika was later surprised to learn, with Rell's, which she did not know about because they had never met, which said something about the universality of certain morning preferences).

She tells Yono about him at the window.

She has told no one before.

He says then we agree.

She does not cry.

She does not write it down.

She files it where it belongs: in the place where things that have been witnessed by someone real live. The same place as the cold floor and the step outside the circle and the sound that came first.

Something witnessed is not the same as something remembered alone.

That distinction is the first time she has been able to think of the outside of the circle as anything other than a mistake.

## CHAPTER 32: Side Story — Lyra's First Wrong Assumption

She was twenty one.

She had been doing consequence line reading for three years. She was good. Everyone told her she was good. She told herself she was good and she had the professional record to support the claim.

She assumed the threat was directional. Coming from the north side. She read the consequence lines and they pointed north and she positioned accordingly and she kept everyone she was responsible for positioned accordingly.

The threat came from the east.

Nobody died directly.

But something that would not have been broken was broken because she had positioned incorrectly and the broken thing could not be repaired and the person the thing mattered to has never fully recovered.

She updates her assumptions every time she is wrong.

She has been wrong four times since then.

This is a low rate, professionally.

It is not low enough.

She will spend the rest of her career trying to make it lower.

She will never reach zero.

She knows this.

She is finding her way toward accepting it while still working toward it. The two things seem contradictory. She has concluded they are not. They are simply both necessary and both true.

She tells Yono nothing of this.

She updates her assumption about him in the forest clearing when he removes the hunger wave's permission and says afterward that was not a new ability and she files the update under active concern and continues being useful.

She catches Tovin's bread before it hits the floor.

She is useful.

She stays useful.

Every update makes her more useful.

She will eventually update her assumption about what useful includes.

## CHAPTER 33: Side Story — Liri's Ribbon

The ribbon was her mother's first.

Sela wore it in her hair when she was young, before Lumera, before the river district, before a man with a bad singing voice and large opinions about nothing important arrived in her life and slowly became the most important thing in it.

When Liri was born, Sela tied the ribbon around her wrist in the old tradition of the river district: a new life, anchored to the people who are already here. A Thread made visible in red cloth. She left it there until Liri's wrist grew too large for it.

Liri was six when she took it back. She wore it in her hair after that, the way her mother used to.

The shade beast attack scattered everything.

She was running. The ribbon came loose. She grabbed it from the air because it was moving in the same direction she was moving and she grabbed it without thinking and then she was running and the ribbon was in her hand and she wrapped it around her wrist without thinking about tradition or the old practice.

She thought about her father.

He was outside, because he told them to hide.

The ribbon was on her wrist and her father was outside and the shadow wolves were in the streets and the red ribbon was on her wrist and she decided it was protection because she needed something that was protection.

She met Yono an hour later.

She was still alive.

She counted this as evidence.

When the threat entity uses the ribbon's shape to knock on Meryn's door, Liri does not know this has happened until after. When she finds out, she touches the ribbon on her wrist for a long moment.

Then she asks Yono to put three layers deep of Thread anchoring on it.

He does.

She wears it like armor after that.

Not because she needs to.

Because it is.

## CHAPTER 34: Side Story — Cadreth's First Empty File

The archive is not a physical place.

Cadreth carries it in the book he holds against his chest, and the book is larger inside than outside in the way that very important books sometimes are. Every file he has ever compiled is in it. Every ending he has ever recorded. Every entity he has assessed, catalogued, and filed under the appropriate classification.

The first time he opened a file and found it empty was three hundred years ago.

Before Velkor. Before the Covenant War. Before the prison door in the forest outside what would eventually become Lumera.

The empty file was not an error. It was an anticipation. He had opened the file because something in the archive told him a record would be needed here. He had opened it and found nothing because the thing the record was for had not yet taken a form he could catalogue.

He left the file open.

He carried it for three hundred years.

The file is the one he arrived in Lumera expecting to close.

When he arrived in the safehouse and Yono told him I am not a warning label, I am a person, Cadreth did something he has not done since the first time he opened that empty file.

He looked at the empty pages.

Then he started writing.

Not a closing record.

A new entry. First of many. The ink in this section of the archive is different from the surrounding entries. Warmer. Like it was written by someone who was genuinely curious about how it would turn out.

The file is labeled with the old notation that indicates: ongoing. Unknown ceiling. Active trajectory.

Cadreth has one hundred and seventeen files with the ongoing notation.

They all have visible endings.

This one does not.

He turned to the last page of the new entry.

It was blank.

He closed the book.

He left the safehouse through the open ceiling.

He is not done writing.

## Glossary of Terms

**Asteria:** The world in which the main events of Threadborn Volume One take place. A world with three landmasses, two inland seas, and three moons of significance. The Thread membrane separating Asteria from the Unnamed Presence is maintained by the collective bonding activity of its inhabitants.

**Anchor Moon:** The stationary moon of Asteria, believed to be the physical expression of the world's largest Thread node convergence point. Glows brightest during periods of high Thread activity.

**Black Hall:** The interior metaphysical space within Yono where his sealed abilities are stored. Experienced visually as a dark corridor with gold cords suspended in it. Each cord corresponds to one sealed ability or state.

**Bloom Absolute:** Violet Arden's most comprehensive combat application. Every surface in her immediate environment blooms with white flowers, each of which is a sealed divine concept she releases simultaneously.

**Cadreth:** The Archivist. The sole keeper of the archive of endings in Asteria. White eyes. Has been carrying Yono's file since before Yono was born.

**Concept (divine):** A fundamental principle of reality that a sufficiently attuned divine being can take ownership of and apply. Violet's power system operates through accumulated concepts.

**Covenant Authority:** The governing body of Thread work in Asteria. Originally a promise between Thread Seers after the Unraveling. Currently an institution with laws, enforcement, and a classification system for dangerous entities.

**Covenant War:** The sixty year conflict three hundred years before Volume One events that ended with the Covenant Authority's dominance and Velkor's imprisonment.

**Crescent:** The black mark in Yono's palm that serves as his Thread interface and the seal anchor for his black hall.

**Hollow Thread:** A severed Thread whose resonance has not yet dissipated. Forms the basis of shade beast construction when manipulated by an external force.

**Lumen Period:** The historical age before the Unraveling when Threads were perceptible to all living things and the world had what scholars call a softer texture.

**Lumera:** A mid sized city in the Settled Continent of Asteria. White walls, red roofs, river district in its lower section. Adjacent to the forest where Velkor's prison door is located.

**Mirror Moon:** The moon of Asteria that orbits normally but reflects the Anchor Moon's light with a slight temporal delay, creating the visual effect of seeing present and recent past simultaneously.

**Rule Maker:** Yono's fundamental ability to identify a rule or property currently applied to a situation and revise it. Requires honesty of intention: the revision must be factually sustainable.

**Seal:** One of the gold cords in Yono's black hall. Each represents a decision he made to put away a version of himself. Breaks when the reason to have it back becomes larger than the reason to keep it sealed.

**Shade Beast:** A broken Thread given form. The aggressive variety is formed from Threads severed by violence. Drawn to living Thread energy. When destroyed, scatters into residue that can be used by the entity that produces them to locate and identify Thread sources.

**Stored Breath:** Meryn's practice of preserving portions of her own Thread energy in small glass jars. Used to supplement her healing reserves when she would otherwise be depleted.

**Thread:** The tangible, manipulable expression of a meaningful connection between two things. The fundamental unit of the power system and the primary structural element of the membrane separating Asteria from the Unnamed Presence.

**Thread Cut:** Yono's recovered ability to sever the link between a force and its purpose at the level of relationship. The force remains but loses its directional intent and dissipates without purpose.

**Thread Seer:** An individual who retains or develops the ability to perceive and interact with the Thread network. Registered and regulated by the Covenant Authority in the Settled Continent.

**Unnamed Presence:** The entity that exists beyond the Thread membrane of Asteria. Not malevolent. Not aware of Asteria's existence. Exerts pressure on the membrane simply by existing at the scale it exists at.

**Velkor:** The Elder. A former Thread Seer who discovered Thread consumption and practiced it for decades before being sealed into a stone prison door in the forest outside Lumera. Imprisoned for three hundred years before the events of Volume One. Defeated by Yono in Chapter Five and returned to the seal.

**Wanderer Moon:** The third moon of Asteria, which follows no predictable orbital pattern. Believed by the Hollow Circle to be a Thread entity large enough to be visible from the surface. Was in an unprecedented position on the night Yono sealed Velkor.

## Known Abilities: Yono Kazeshima

**Time Control:** Slows the subjective experience of time in Yono's awareness. The world does not physically slow. His perception of it does, allowing him to move and respond within the gap. Note: fear does not slow with the world. He remains afraid in real time.

**Regeneration:** Closes wounds by running the healing process at significantly accelerated rates. Not painless. Not infinite in resource cost: requires the body's own material and generates fatigue proportional to the severity of what it is repairing.

**Damage Denial:** Active. Allows Yono to choose whether to register incoming damage. A hit lands but only becomes injury if he permits it. Requires awareness of the hit to apply.

**Rule Maker:** Allows identification and revision of rules, properties, or permissions applied to a situation. Requires honesty of intention. Cannot revise fundamental truths. Applies to situational rules rather than absolute realities.

**Pre Attack Sight:** Allows perception of attacks before they are physically launched, registered as the reading of an attacker's intention rather than the mechanics of the attack itself. Also applies to non physical attacks: he registered Velkor's approach before Velkor announced himself.

**Thread Cut:** Severs the link between a force and its purpose at the relational level. The force continues to exist but loses its meaning and direction. Does not require physical proximity to the Thread being cut.

**Bloom Absolute:** Full simultaneous release of all currently available concepts. Each concept manifests as a white flower on nearby surfaces before release.

**Active Concepts (Documented):** Time Halt, Gravity Reversal, Concept Lock, Spiritual Severance, and seventeen additional concepts without currently known names in any spoken language.

## Timeline of Asteria

HISTORICAL PERIOD — Age of First Weaving (Lumen Period)

Threads visible to all living things. Reality described as soft in texture. Thread network a universal feature rather than a specialized practice.

THE UNRAVELING — Exact date unknown

Thread network collapses to near silence. Cause disputed. Result: most inhabitants lose Thread perception permanently. Small number retain attenuated sensitivity.

EARLY SECOND AGE — Approximately 200 years after the Unraveling

Thread Seers find each other. Original Covenant formed. Two sentence promise. Beginning of organized Thread work.

THE COVENANT WAR — Approximately 400 years before present

Velkor breaks from the Covenant. Thread consumption discovered and practiced. Conflict spanning three continents and sixty years. Velkor sealed by five senior Thread Seers. One dies in the process. Covenant Authority established as governing body.

LATE AUTHORITY PERIOD — The following 300 years

Velkor in prison. Covenant Authority governance of Thread work expands. Thread monitor stones installed across the Settled Continent. Some stones in the older districts of Lumera cease accurate reporting. No corrective action taken.

PRESENT — Events of Volume One

Yono Kazeshima dies on a Tokyo bridge.

Wakes up in Asteria.

Runs toward the scream.

Finds the room he said he wanted to deserve.

The seals are breaking.

The latest Yono is the strongest Yono.

Last chapter's ceiling is the new floor.

One more reason.

Always one more reason.
