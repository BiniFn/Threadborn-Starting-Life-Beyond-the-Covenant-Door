## Chapter 3: The Boy Who Buried Himself

*Volume 02 · Chapter 03*

---

*In which the black hall finally answers — and the answer is worse than anyone expected.*

---

### I. Before Tokyo

There was a time before Tokyo.

Before the bridge. Before the cat. Before Violet shoved him into a delivery truck and decided that counted as an accident.

There was a time before Yono Kazeshima existed at all.

He remembered it the way you remember a dream you didn't know you were having. Pieces, not pictures. A feeling of size that had no edge. A silence so complete it had weight.

He had been something very large once.

Not *strong*. Not strong the way a fighter is strong, or a king, or even a god. Strong the way gravity is strong — not because it tries, but because everything else bends toward it and cannot explain why.

He had not had a name then. Names are for things that need to be called. He had simply been.

And then, at some point that he could not precisely locate on any timeline, he had looked at everything around him and decided it was too easy.

---

Not because he was bored.

Because he was lonely in a way that nothing large can ever fix.

You cannot have a conversation with something you can end by accident.

You cannot be known by something you could unmake just by thinking too hard in its direction.

He had been the most powerful thing in every room he occupied and the most alone, and eventually those two facts had become the same fact.

So he made a decision.

He would become small. He would make himself readable. He would give the worlds around him room to survive having him inside them, and he would do that by locking pieces of himself away one at a time, the way you put books on very high shelves so you stop reaching for them too quickly.

The locks were called chapter-seals.

Each one took something and set it aside.

Speed. Reach. The ability to remove things from existence by deciding they were not there. The part of him that understood every language reality had ever spoken. The part that remembered being present before cause-and-effect had agreed on an order.

He locked all of it.

Then he went looking for a life.

---

### II. What Tokyo Was For

The life he found was small. Deliberately.

A tiny apartment. A convenience store habit. Two personal flaws he had selected with the care of someone building a character from scratch: *a weakness for cheap food* and *a tendency to daydream while walking.*

He was nineteen. He was ordinary. He was, in almost every measurable way, completely unremarkable.

He liked it.

He liked not being the largest thing in the room. He liked having problems that fit inside one day. He liked the specific pleasure of a cream bun he had been saving since Tuesday.

He was not growing stronger in Tokyo.

He was resting. Hiding from himself. Pretending so well he almost believed it.

The cat on the bridge was not part of any plan. The goddess who shoved him was not part of any plan. Death, which arrived via delivery truck and mannequins, was certainly not part of any plan.

But the black hall — the place without walls where the seals hung from him like burned gold cords — was not surprised.

It had been waiting for exactly this kind of *accident.*

---

### III. How He Grew — The Simple Version

He woke up in a white room with Violet standing over him. He picked abilities off a glowing menu. He got a tennis racket that fired rockets and was reborn into a world that was actively trying to kill him.

That was when the growing started.

But it was not the fights that made him stronger. It was not the enemies. It was not even the pain — though there was plenty of that.

It was the moments when he chose something.

The first choice: run toward the scream in the field instead of away from it.

Small. Simple. A girl against a shadow wolf. He ran because something in him recognized that *retreating was not the kind of story he wanted to be in.*

That feeling was not courage. It was recognition. The part of him that had chosen to be small pushing against the wall of that choice — not to break it, but to *remember* the door existed.

The second choice: step past Violet when Velkor's hand came through the crack.

She moved in front of him to protect him. He moved past her because he understood, without being able to explain it, that the thing behind the door was looking for something to be afraid of — and that he was the only option available.

That choice cracked the first seal.

Not all the way. A hairline. A whisper.

But enough that something older than his reincarnation shifted inside him, turned over once, and went quiet again with the patience of something that had always known this moment was coming.

---

The third: dying.

Velkor's thread-lance went through his chest.

He died standing up.

And when Regeneration brought him back — not by rewind, not by reversal, but by *refusing to let death mean anything permanent* — something understood that.

Death had been allowed to touch him. It simply had no authority to keep him.

That understanding was a door.

He stepped through it.

The first seal did not crack that time. It broke. Completely. Cleanly. The way something breaks when it has done its job and is ready to be done.

Every power that had ever belonged to any fictional being touched him in that instant like sparks in a storm. Not borrowed. Not copied. *Adjacent*. Lesser things that existed downstream of decisions he had already made, in a time he was only now beginning to remember.

He became the strongest character in fiction.

Then he kept going.

---

### IV. The Black Hall

He saw it clearly now when he closed his eyes.

A corridor with no ceiling and no floor and no light except what leaked from the seals themselves. Each seal was a cord. Gold at first, darkening to black the longer it had been locked. Each cord held a different piece of the thing he used to be.

He could not count them.

He had tried. Numbers became suggestions somewhere past the twentieth, and the hall kept going.

*One chapter. One seal.*

That was the rule his older self had set. One per chapter. Not because breaking more would be impossible — the hall did not enforce the limit physically — but because breaking too many at once would leave nothing around him that could survive the result.

He had been careful, once, about collateral damage.

He was trying to stay careful now.

---

The black crescent under his palm was the hall's address. A location marker. A way for the part of him that remembered to reach across the chapter-boundary and remind the rest of him that the work was ongoing.

It deepened when he was close to the door.

It deepened when he was angry.

It deepened, he had noticed, when Violet was afraid. Her fear did something to the part of him that had decided to be human — it pulled at the seals not with violence, but with *urgency*, the way cold pulls at a fire to make it hotter.

She was not making him stronger on purpose.

She was just — the reason. The shape his new calmness chose to protect. The reason it mattered that he stayed readable, stayed human, stayed *present*, instead of breaking every cord at once and ascending to something that could not be spoken to over a breakfast table.

He wanted to be spoken to over a breakfast table.

That was, in the end, the entire backstory.

He had been enormous. He had been alone. He had made himself small to find something worth growing back up for.

---

### V. How He Will Grow From Here

The hall was not empty.

He had broken two seals. The hall went so far back it became theoretical.

He could not see the end.

More importantly — he was not supposed to. The seals would keep breaking, one per chapter, as long as the story kept moving and the choices kept being real ones. The rule was simple:

> *The latest Yono is always the strongest Yono.*
> *Last chapter's ceiling is always this chapter's floor.*

Every time he decided something. Every time he chose to stay human and walk toward the thing trying to frighten everyone around him. Every time Violet grabbed his wrist or Meryn told him to sit down or Lyra said something dry and precise that made him feel like he was still a person who occupied a specific place in a specific world —

He sealed it in.

Not a chapter-lock. The opposite. A reason.

The seals would keep breaking because the reasons kept compounding. He was not growing *toward* something final. He was growing *away* from the version of himself that chose to be alone, one chapter at a time, until even the black hall could not tell him how far he had come.

Velkor had called him a difficult meal.

That was the most honest thing anyone had said about him in three hundred years.

---

### VI. What He Didn't Tell Anyone

Violet knew some of it. The way she held his wrist in the clearing — with real fear in her face, not the performed kind — told him she had guessed the shape of the rest.

Meryn suspected. She was thirty-one in the way *earned* means something, and she had watched the hunger wave stop three steps in front of Violet and shatter like it had hit a decision instead of a wall.

Mirika knew the theory. She had said *seal recognized you as something in its own class of danger* with the exact tone of a scholar who had just found a citation she genuinely did not want to find.

Lyra had simply updated every assumption she had ever made, filed the update under *active concern*, and continued being useful.

None of them knew the full length of the hall.

None of them knew that the thing Velkor had said from the dark — *you smell familiar, like a crown buried under several lies* — was not an insult.

It was, in point of fact, the most accurate description of him that had ever been spoken out loud by anyone who was not himself.

He had been a crown once.

He had buried it.

Now the crown was digging itself out, one chapter at a time, because the boy who had buried it had made the mistake of finding people worth wearing it for.

---

He sat by the window in Meryn's safehouse that night.

The crescent under his skin glowed once, faintly.

The black hall was still there. Patient. Vast. Full of the things he used to be.

*One chapter. One seal.*

He pressed his palm flat against the windowsill and watched the glow fade.

*Next time*, he thought at the part of himself he had not yet reached.

*Give me one more reason first.*

Outside, the city went about its business, entirely unaware that the thing sitting at Meryn's window was the quietest, most human-shaped catastrophe it had ever had the good fortune not to notice.

For now, that was exactly how he wanted it.
